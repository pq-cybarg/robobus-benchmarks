The four classes, grounded in NIST. classical is reserved for the quantum-broken asymmetric algorithms — RSA, DH, ECDH, ECDSA, Ed25519 — which Shor breaks outright and NIST IR 8547 lists as quantum-vulnerable (deprecate 2030, disallow 2035). PQC is the NIST post-quantum asymmetric replacement (FIPS 203 ML-KEM, FIPS 204 ML-DSA). HYBRID runs a classical and a PQC primitive together (CNSA 2.0 transition). QR covers symmetric / hash / MAC / KDF: a quantum computer does not break these — only Grover's quadratic speedup applies — and NIST defines its five PQ strength Categories BY them: Cat 1 = AES-128, 2 = SHA-256, 3 = AES-192, 4 = SHA-384, 5 = AES-256. So AES-128 and SHA-256 are quantum-security levels, not "classical" — even HMAC/HKDF/PBKDF2 over SHA-256 stay 128-bit quantum-safe (PRFs, not collision-bound). Each row's green note carries the NIST Category and CNSA-2.0 fitness; CNSA 2.0 mandates the Level-5 tier (AES-256, SHA-384, ML-KEM-1024, ML-DSA-87). AES-128 (Level 1) is the QR floor — anything weaker (3DES 112-bit, DES, RC4, MD5, SHA-1) is sub-threshold and stays classical.
A second, orthogonal axis — construction & role (the green notes in each row). Two primitives can share a quantum class yet differ structurally: SHA3/SHAKE/KMAC use the Keccak sponge, which is length-extension-immune — so KMAC (SP 800-185) keys a hash directly with no HMAC nesting, and it's design-diverse from SHA-2's Merkle–Damgård (which is length-extendable, hence HMAC). That is not a quantum-status difference — SHA-384/512 and SHA3-384/512 are equally QR. Likewise Argon2id is not "just a KDF": it is a memory-hard passphrase KDF for low-entropy inputs (GPU/ASIC-resistant), a different role from HKDF/KMAC-KDF, which are fast extractors for high-entropy inputs, and stronger than PBKDF2 (iterations only, no memory-hardness). The Class badge is the quantum axis; the green note is the construction/role axis.
